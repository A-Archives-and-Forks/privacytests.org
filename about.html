What are the Browser Privacy Tests?

Arthur Edelstein, September 29, 2021

All web browsers leak, but some are more leaky than others. Importantly, most browsers leak your identity and your browser history. The website reports the results of an ongoing audit of the major privacy leaks of web browsers.

Why web privacy matters

The web has rapidly developed into one of the primary ways that billions of people interact with the world. Reading the news, communication with friends and colleagues, entertainment, participating in political discourse, and searching for information all take place largely through the web.

Despite the crucial importance of the web, it has been designed in a fashion that historically has not respected the individual human right to privacy. Numerous Web standards are actively hostile to preserving users' anonymity as they browse the web.

Why is this a problem? Because it violates your privacy, and allows you to be discriminated against. This is not merely creepy, but a danger to our individual human rights and the structure of free societies.

Why browsers are crucial

Once data has left your laptop, tablet or phone, there is not much you can do to control it. Companies are happy to hold on to data for years or decades. But how did it leave your device? More often than not, that data has leaked through a web browser.

Web browsers, sometimes inadvertently and but often by design, let tracking companies know what you read, what you write, where you live, what you search for and what you buy. And this information is assembled by those companies into detailed individual profiles of every person on the internet: these companies know all about your ethnicity, your religion, your politic views, your sexual orientation, your gender, your family, your friends, your colleagues, your health history, your relationships, your educational records, and so on -- all data leaked through web browsers.

Fortunately, most browser makers have recognized their responsibility to protect users' privacy, and are working on improving their privacy protections. But the default behavior for browsers used by billions of people remains highly leaky. For most people using the web, massive amounts of data continues to be silently leaked by their web browsers.

How the tests work

I wanted to understand in detail: what data is each browser leaking? What browsers are best for privacy?

In order to understand and compare what the privacy characteristics of browsers are, I subject each browser to the same suite of open source automated tests. Each privacy test emulates a different method of tracking. After each tracking test runs, it records whether that tracking attempt succeeded in each browser. For example, our testing apparatus will tell a browser to visit two test websites that embed the same third-party cookie. If that cookie allows information to be shared between the two websites, we know the browser is not protecting you against generic third-party cookie tracking.

If a browser is found to protect users from the data leak, it gets a green , but if it leaks user data, it gets a red .

For more technical details are available in the technical section.

It is my hope that these comparative tests will help you decide which browser to use. And I hope it will encourage browser makers to continue to improve the privacy protections of their browsers. The tests will continue to run with the latest version of each browser and the results will be updated on the homepage.

This project is a work in progress! Not all tracking vectors are included in our tests. In the future I expect to add more missing tracking vectors. I plan to include more browsers and browser extensions in our tests. Feedback, bug reports and pull requests are welcome.
