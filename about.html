What is PrivacyTests.org?

Arthur Edelstein

All web browsers leak, but some are more leaky than others. Most browsers leak your identity and your browser history. This website reports the results of an ongoing audit of the privacy characteristics of several important web browsers.

Why privacy matters

The web has rapidly developed into one of the primary ways that billions of people interact with the world. Reading the news, communicating with friends and colleagues, watching movies, participating in political discourse, and searching for information all take place largely through the web.

Despite the crucial importance of the web, it has been designed in a fashion that historically has not respected individuals' human right to privacy. Numerous web standards are actively hostile to preserving users' anonymity.

Why is this a problem? Because it violates your privacy, and allows you to be discriminated against. This is not merely creepy, but a danger to our human rights and the structure of free societies.

Why web browsers are critical to online privacy

Once private data has left your laptop, tablet or phone, there is not much you can do to control it. Companies are happy to hold on to your data for years or decades. But how did that data leave your device? More often than not, that data has leaked through a web browser.

Web browsers, sometimes inadvertently but often by design, leak data about what websites you have visited. This information lets tracking companies know what you read, what you write, where you are located, what you search for and what you buy. And this highly personal information is assembled by those companies into detailed individual profiles of every person on the internet. These companies know all about your ethnicity, your religion, your politic views, your sexual orientation, your gender, your family, your friends, your colleagues, your health history, your habits, your relationships, your educational records, and so on. And companies often will sell and share the data to third parties.

Fortunately, some browser makers have recognized their responsibility to stop users' private information from leaking out in the first place. All browser teams are working on improving their privacy protections. But the default behavior for browsers used by billions of people remains highly leaky. For most people using the web, massive amounts of data continues to be silently leaked by their web browsers.

How the tests work

The goal of PrivacyTests.org is to understand in detail: what data is each browser leaking? What browsers are best for privacy?

In order to understand and compare what the privacy characteristics of browsers are, we subject each browser to the same suite of open-source automated tests. Each privacy test emulates a different method of tracking. After each tracking test runs, it records whether that tracking attempt succeeded in each browser. For example, our testing system will instruct a browser to visit two test websites that embed the same third-party cookie. If the browser allows that cookie to be shared between the two websites, we know the browser is failing to protect its users against third-party cookie tracking.

If a browser is found to protect users from the data leak, it gets a green , but if it leaks user data, it gets a red .

It is my hope that these comparative tests will help you decide which browser to use. And I hope it will encourage browser makers to continue to improve the privacy protections of their browsers. The tests will continue to run with the latest version of each browser and the results will be updated on the homepage.

Work in progress

This project is a work in progress! Not all tracking vectors are included in our tests. In the future I expect to add more many more privacy tests. Feedback, bug reports and pull requests are welcome.
